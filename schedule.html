Operating System Scheduling Algorithms

First Come First Serve (FCFS)

Non-preemptive Simple

First Come First Serve (FCFS)

Executes processes in the order they arrive in the ready queue.

  • Advantages: Simple to implement, fair for long processes
  • Disadvantages: Can lead to convoy effect, high average waiting time
  • Use case: Batch systems where request order is important

Shortest Job First (SJF)

Can be preemptive or non-preemptive Optimal for average waiting time

Shortest Job First (SJF)

Selects the process with the smallest execution time to execute next.

  • Advantages: Minimizes average waiting time, good for short processes
  • Disadvantages: Can lead to starvation of longer processes, requires knowing/estimating process execution times
  • Use case: Batch systems where job lengths are known in advance

Round Robin (RR)

Preemptive Fair allocation of CPU

Round Robin (RR)

Allocates a fixed time unit per process in a cyclic way.

  • Advantages: Fair allocation of CPU, good for time-sharing systems
  • Disadvantages: Performance depends on time quantum size, higher context switch overhead
  • Use case: Time-sharing systems, interactive systems

Priority Scheduling

Can be preemptive or non-preemptive Based on priority values

Priority Scheduling

Selects the process with the highest priority for execution.

  • Advantages: Can prioritize important processes, flexible
  • Disadvantages: Can lead to starvation of low-priority processes, priority inversion problem
  • Use case: Real-time systems, systems where process importance varies
